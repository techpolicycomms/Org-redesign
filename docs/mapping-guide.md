# Role Mapping Guide: From Hierarchy to Intelligence

This guide translates common role archetypes from a traditional hierarchy into the three roles of the intelligence-era org: **IC**, **DRI**, and **Player-Coach**. For each archetype it estimates how much of the role is *information routing* (replaceable by a world model), *edge contact* (direct exposure to customers, markets, or reality), and *building* (deepening a capability, interface, or person).

The percentages are defensible defaults drawn from observing hundreds of org charts. They are the starting point for a conversation, not a verdict. A specific person in a specific company will differ — which is exactly what the diagnostic engine in this repo is for.

## How to read the table

- **Primary activity** — what the role actually does on a typical week, stripped of the job title.
- **Likely new role** — the archetype the role collapses into once routing is removed. "Eliminated / absorbed" means the work does not go away, but it moves into the world model or is absorbed by ICs and DRIs.
- **System layer** — where the remaining work lives in the 4-layer architecture.
- **Notes** — the interesting edge case. Most of the nuance is here.

## Taxonomy

| Current Role Archetype | Primary Activity | Likely New Role | System Layer | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **VP (Eng / Product / etc.)** | Compressing the state of a large org for the CEO; allocating headcount; owning OKRs | DRI *(for specific cross-cutting problems)* or Eliminated / absorbed | Intelligence | ~70% routing, ~10% edge, ~20% building. When the world model compresses state natively, the VP role fragments: the person becomes a time-boxed DRI on the 2–3 bets that actually need a single throat, and the rest of the job disappears. Most VPs will resist this because their leverage used to *be* the compression. |
| **Director** | Running a function within a function; translating strategy down and status up | Eliminated / absorbed; best ones become Player-Coaches | Intelligence | ~75% routing, ~5% edge, ~20% building/people. The director layer is the purest expression of hierarchy-as-routing and takes the largest hit. Directors who were secretly still building (code, designs, models) land cleanly as Player-Coaches. Directors who stopped building five years ago have the hardest transition. |
| **Engineering Manager** | 1:1s, sprint ceremonies, unblocking, career ladders, hiring | Player-Coach | Capability | ~45% routing, ~10% edge, ~45% people/building. The good EM already works like a player-coach; the redesign just removes the status-meeting tax from their calendar. Span of care drops from 8 reports to 4–5, because without routing tax a single person can actually go deep with each of them. |
| **Product Manager** | Writing PRDs, running standups, compressing customer feedback into roadmaps, chasing engineers | IC *(world-model curator)* or DRI *(for a launch)* | Intelligence / WorldModel | ~55% routing, ~25% edge, ~20% building. The PM role bifurcates sharply. The "status PM" disappears. The "world-model PM" — the person who actually keeps the representation of the customer accurate and deep — becomes one of the most valuable ICs in the company. PMs running a specific launch become time-boxed DRIs. |
| **TPM (Technical Program Manager)** | Tracking dependencies, managing launch checklists, running cross-team standups | Eliminated / absorbed; rare best cases become DRIs | Intelligence | ~85% routing, ~5% edge, ~10% building. The job *is* routing. When the world model tracks dependencies natively and an intelligence layer schedules work against them, the TPM role is the one that goes away most completely. The exception: TPMs who are secretly the only person who understands how a hard system fits together — they are ICs on the capability layer and should be renamed accordingly. |
| **Staff Engineer** | Designing systems, unblocking teams, reviewing architecture, writing some code | IC | Capability | ~20% routing, ~10% edge, ~70% building. Already very close to the IC archetype. The redesign mostly *protects* staff engineers from the routing tax they accumulate as they become senior ("can you just come to this review"). Their capabilities become first-class, owned, versioned. |
| **Data Scientist** | Building models, running analyses, answering ad-hoc questions from execs | IC *(world-model builder)* | WorldModel | ~25% routing, ~15% edge, ~60% building. Data scientists are the people literally building the world model. In most orgs they spend a third of their time being a human query interface for executives — that routing evaporates when the world model is directly queryable. What remains is the deep building. |
| **Designer** | Research, prototyping, design reviews, critique, handing off to engineers | IC | Interface | ~15% routing, ~35% edge, ~50% building. Designers live closer to the edge than almost anyone in the company — they see users directly, and they ship the thing users actually touch. The redesign barely changes their day job; it mostly removes the design-review meeting tax and puts them on the Interface layer as first-class owners. |
| **Operations Manager** | Process design, vendor management, incident response, reporting | DRI or Player-Coach | Capability / Intelligence | ~55% routing, ~20% edge, ~25% building. Ops managers are some of the most under-credited people in hierarchies — they absorb chaos so other people don't have to. Post-redesign, the process-design part becomes building capabilities (first-class, owned). The reporting part disappears. They usually land as DRIs for specific operational problems. |
| **HR Business Partner** | 1:1s with managers, performance cycle administration, employee relations | Player-Coach *(rare)* or Eliminated / absorbed | Interface | ~70% routing, ~15% edge, ~15% building. Most HRBP time is routing between employees and policies and between managers and HR systems. When the world model owns the policies and the systems are self-serve, the job collapses. The remaining role is narrow, senior, and judgement-heavy: the person who handles the 5% of situations that are actually hard. |
| **Project Coordinator** | Calendaring, status updates, document wrangling, meeting notes | Eliminated / absorbed | — | ~95% routing, ~0% edge, ~5% building. The role that disappears most completely. The work does not disappear — it moves into the world model, which tracks state without needing a human to chase it. The individuals usually have strong reality-testing instincts and often make excellent DRIs on their next problem. |
| **Customer Success Manager** | QBRs, renewal conversations, escalation handling, account health monitoring | IC *(edge)* or DRI | Edge / Interface | ~35% routing, ~50% edge, ~15% building. Customer success is mostly edge contact wrapped in a lot of reporting ceremony. Strip the reporting and the role is one of the most valuable in the new org: the humans who actually know what customers are trying to do. Account health monitoring moves to the world model. The human stays on the edge. |

## Reading the percentages

The three columns (routing / edge / building) always sum to 100. A fourth dimension — *people development* — is embedded in the "building" number for Player-Coach candidates, since in this framework growing another human is a form of building capability.

A rough rule: **if routing > 50% and building < 20%, the role is the one that gets eliminated or absorbed most aggressively.** Those are the cells most at risk of discovering that their job was, in fact, the routing layer. That's not a failure of the person — it's the point of the redesign. The people who were best at routing are usually excellent DRIs on their next problem, because they already know where the system's joints are.
